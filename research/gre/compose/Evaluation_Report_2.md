## 四、 6.0 分高分范文 (Band 6.0 Sample Essay)
**题目回溯：** Society should make efforts to save endangered species only if the potential extinction of those species is the result of human activities.

**范文：**
The proposition suggests a pragmatic, cause-oriented approach to wildlife conservation: society should exclusively focus its resources on saving endangered species whose potential demise is attributable to human activities. At first glance, this view appears reasonable, as it emphasizes moral restitution for humanity’s ecological transgressions while implicitly acknowledging the finite nature of conservation resources. However, a deeper examination reveals that this criterion is fundamentally flawed. Not only is it practically impossible to disentangle human influence from natural extinction in the modern era, but ignoring so-called "naturally" endangered species also undermines the intricate web of biodiversity upon which all life, including our own, critically depends.

Admittedly, the rationale behind the prompt stems from a valid sense of moral accountability. Since the dawn of the Industrial Revolution, human actions—such as deforestation, overhunting, and pollution—have artificially accelerated the extinction rate to hundreds of times the historical baseline. It seems ethically sound that humanity should shoulder the responsibility of rectifying its own mistakes. Furthermore, considering that governmental and philanthropic budgets for wildlife protection are perpetually constrained, prioritizing species directly victimized by human exploitation, such as the critically endangered rhinoceros or the Amazonian orangutan, presents a realistic strategy for resource allocation. 

Nevertheless, the premise falls apart when confronting the reality of the Anthropocene epoch—a geological era defined by pervasive human impact. Today, the boundary between "human-induced" and "natural" extinction has become inextricably blurred. Anthropogenic climate change, ocean acidification, and the global spread of microplastics have altered the very fabric of the biosphere. If a species of amphibian is facing extinction due to a fungal disease, is it a natural occurrence, or was the rapid spread of the fungus facilitated by rising global temperatures caused by human carbon emissions? Because human influence has permeated virtually every corner of the globe, attempting to isolate pure "natural" extinctions to exempt ourselves from conservation efforts is an exercise in futility.

Moreover, the intrinsic interconnectedness of ecosystems dictates that we cannot afford to neglect any species based on the origin of its endangerment. Ecosystems are not random assortments of isolated organisms, but rather complex, interdependent networks. The disappearance of a single species, regardless of why it vanishes, can trigger a trophic cascade that destabilizes the entire ecosystem. For instance, the extinction of a pollinator insect due to a naturally occurring virus could lead to the collapse of specific plant populations, which in turn starves the herbivores relying on those plants. Ultimately, human agriculture and food security rely heavily on these robust, biodiverse ecosystems. Protecting biodiversity must be a holistic endeavor; selectively saving species based on a causality test is ecologically myopic.

Finally, biodiversity possesses intrinsic scientific and medical value that transcends the cause of endangerment. Countless species harbor genetic secrets that have led to monumental medical breakthroughs. The venom of a rare snake or the chemical compounds in an obscure rainforest plant might hold the key to curing cancer or Alzheimer's disease. If society stood idly by while such species perished merely because their decline was "natural," humanity would be permanently depriving itself of invaluable scientific discoveries. Our stewardship of the planet should be guided by a forward-looking recognition of the inherent value of all life forms, rather than a backward-looking assignment of blame.

In conclusion, while allocating conservation efforts based on human culpability seems like an intuitively fair way to balance the budget and morality, it is a misguided and perilous strategy. The pervasive reach of human activity makes such distinctions impossible, and the fragile interdependence of ecosystems makes them dangerous. Society’s commitment to preserving endangered species must be universal, driven not by the narrow criteria of guilt, but by the profound realization that in saving the diversity of life on Earth, we are ultimately saving ourselves.
